## Exercises and Games
**Yes, And (Haan, aur)**
- The "Yes, And..." improv game is a fundamental exercise in improvisational theater. It's all about building a scene collaboratively with your fellow performers. 
- The goal of the game is to create a scene where each participant responds positively to what the previous person has said or done, adding new information and ideas to build upon the scene.
- **Start with an Offer:** One person starts the scene by making an offer. This could be a simple statement, that establishes the basic premise of the scene. 
- **Accept and Add:** The next person in the scene accepts the offer by responding positively with a "Yes, And..." statement. This means they agree with what was established and then add something new to the scene. 
- **Keep It Going:** Continue the scene by taking turns accepting and adding to each other's contributions. The key is to always say "Yes, And..." and avoid contradicting or denying what has been established. This keeps the scene flowing and prevents it from stalling.
- **Build and Heighten:** As the scene progresses, you can gradually build upon the initial premise and make it more interesting or absurd. For example, you might introduce a quirky customer who demands a cake shaped like a giraffe.
- **Stay in Character:** Try to stay in character and maintain consistency within the scene. Develop your character's personality, motivations, and relationships with others in the scene.
- **Listen Actively:** Pay close attention to what your scene partners are saying and doing. This will help you respond in a way that adds to the scene and makes it more engaging.
- **End the Scene:** The scene can naturally come to an end when you feel like you've explored the premise or when you reach a satisfying conclusion. You can also initiate an ending by making a statement that wraps things up. 


**What are you doing? (Improv game)**
- The goal of the game is to create a humorous and entertaining exchange of actions and responses among the players.
- Starting Player: The starting player steps into the center of the circle and begins an action, such as miming a simple activity or motion (e.g., brushing their teeth).
- Question: While performing the action, the starting player turns to another player in the circle and asks, "What are you doing?" The player being asked should respond with an action that is entirely different from what the starting player is actually doing. For example, if the starting player is miming brushing their teeth, the responding player might say, "I'm juggling flaming torches."
- **Imitate and Continue:** After hearing the response, the starting player must immediately stop their initial action and start miming the new action mentioned by the responding player (e.g., juggling flaming torches).
- **Repeat:** The player who originally responded with the new action then turns to someone else in the circle, asks, "What are you doing?" and the game continues with a new action and response.
- **No Repetition:** Players should avoid repeating actions that have already been used in the game. The goal is to come up with unique and unexpected actions, which adds to the humor and creativity of the game.
- **Timing:** Participants should aim for a quick exchange of actions and responses. If a player hesitates for too long or repeats an action, they can be eliminated from the game, or you can simply continue without them.
- **Keep It Funny:** The game is meant to be lighthearted and humorous, so encourage players to be imaginative and come up with funny or absurd actions and responses.
- **Ending the Game:** You can play for a set amount of time or until the group decides to end the game. There's no strict winner or loser in this game; it's all about having fun and being creative.
